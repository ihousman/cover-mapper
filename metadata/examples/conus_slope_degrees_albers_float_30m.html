Abstract:
Slope is defined as the first derivative or the rate of change of a function at a certain point. Even though the mathematical equation for calculating slope is well-defined, how to apply that equation to gridded elevation data varies (Sharpnack and Akin 1969, Fleming and Hoffer 1979, Horn 1981, Unwin 1981, O'Callaghan and Mark 1984, Zevenbergen and Thorn 1987, Wood, 1996). The method employed by both ArcGIS and ERDAS is a third-order finite difference method (Horn 1981), which will hereafter be referred to as Horns slope algorithm. Skidmore (1989) compared Horns slope algorithm along with five others methods to true slopes calculated manually from 50 sites. Horns slope algorithm was found to correlate strongly with true slope. There were two other methods that correlated strongly with true slope, but tests showed little difference between these three methods. Zhou and Liu (2004a, 2004b) also compared six slope algorithms, which included Horns slope algorithm, to true slopes obtained from a synthetically derived surface. Five of the algorithms compared favorably with the true slopes and there were very little difference between the results from the five algorithms. The rate of change in the horizontal and vertical directions from the center cell determines the slope. The basic algorithm used to calculate slope is Slope (Degrees)=atan(sqrt([dz/dx]^2 + [dz/dy]^2)) * (180/pi) (Horn 1981). For the 3x3 window below, dz/dx and dz/dy are calculated as shown. a b c d e f g h i dz/dx=((c+2f+i) - (a+2d+g)) / (8*CellSize) dz/dy=((g+2h+i) - (a+2b+c)) / (8*CellSize) It is well known that slope is directly related to erosion and water flow, which influences various soil characteristics, which influences vegetation composition and growth (Anderson and Anderson 2010, Ellison 1947, Lane et al. 1995, Liu and Singh 2004, Perring 1959). Slope has been used extensively in image classification with various results (Strahler et al. 1978, Fleming and Hoffer 1979, Tom and Miller 1980, Justice et al 1981, Hutchinson 1982, Franklin et al. 1986, Cibula and Nyquist 1987, Johnston 1987, Skidmore 1989, Royle and Lathrop 2000, Doucette et al. 2009, Dobrowski et al. 2008, Ren et al. 2009).
Purpose:
To provide a CONUS wide dataset of slope at 30 m spatial resolution.
