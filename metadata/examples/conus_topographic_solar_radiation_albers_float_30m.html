Abstract:
Aspect is a poor variable to use for quantitative analyses because an aspect of 1° is very similar to an aspect of 360°. A solution to this problem is to transform aspect. The DEM derivatives suite of programs include three methods to transform aspect: slope-aspect transformation using cosine, slope-aspect transformation using sine, and topographic solar-radiation index. The topographic solar-radiation index is a transformation of aspect that assigns 0s to northeasterly aspects and 1s to southwesterly aspects (Roberts and Cooper 1989, Evans 2004a). Topographic Solar-Radiation Index = ( 1 - cos ( ( PI / 180 ) × ( Aspect - 30 ) ) ) / 2 Transformations of aspect are indirect measures of solar radiation. For the country of France, Piedallu and Gégout (2008) compared five methods, including sine transformation of aspect, cosine transformation of aspect, and cosine transformation of aspect multiplied by slope, to actual ground measured solar radiation values. They found that none of the five methods were correlated with ground solar radiation values (R2 = 0.02). They also compared the abilities of these five methods to predict plant distributions for the country of France and also for smaller geographic areas. They found for the country of France, the models had low predictive abilities, but for smaller geographic areas, the predictive abilities increased. They suggest the reason why other studies have been successful in using various forms of these transformation methods to predict plant distributions is because they were carried out on small areas. Aspect transformations have been used successfully to predict tree growth, basal area, forest productivity, and species composition (Stage 1976, Stage and Salas 2007, Roise and Betters 1981, Villwock et al. 2011, Dobrowski et al. 2008). Evans and Cushman (2009) found Pinus ponderosa occurrences were influenced by the topographic solar-radiation index.
Purpose:
To provide a CONUS wide dataset of topographic solar radiation at 30 m spatial resolution.
